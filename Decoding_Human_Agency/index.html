The Libet Experiment: When Do You Decide?

In the 1980s, Benjamin Libet made a startling discovery: when you freely decide to move your finger, your brain shows activity (called "readiness potential") about 350ms before you consciously feel the urge to move.

This sparked decades of debate: If your brain "decides" before you're aware, are you really in control?

The free will debate continues: When does an action become "yours"?

But Wait‚ÄîThere's Another Critical Moment

Try this: Decide to move your finger, but at the last moment, try to stop. There's a point‚Äîabout 200 milliseconds before you move‚Äîwhen it's too late to cancel. Your brain has already committed. What if we could detect that exact moment? And what if that moment is when you start feeling "I did that"?

The Mystery of "I Did That"

When you do something voluntarily‚Äîlike pressing a button‚Äîyou feel like you caused it. That feeling of control is called your sense of agency. But when exactly does your brain decide "this action is mine"?

The Point of No Return

Research shows there's a critical moment‚Äîroughly 200 milliseconds before you physically move‚Äîwhen you can no longer cancel the action. It's like your brain has already said "we're doing this" even though your finger hasn't moved yet. This project asks: Is that the moment when you start to feel in control?

The Hypothesis

Once you cross the point of no return, your brain might treat the action as "already happening"‚Äîeven before you move. So if something happens in that brief window, you should still feel responsible for it.
