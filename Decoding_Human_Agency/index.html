Do You Really Control Your Actions?

One of the most provocative experiments in neuroscience‚Äîthe Libet experiment‚Äîshowed that your brain activity predicts your decisions before you're consciously aware of them. Does this mean free will is an illusion? Or is there more to the story?

üéØ Project Overview

This research project investigates the neural basis of human agency and voluntary action through real-time brain-computer interfaces. We're building on decades of neuroscientific research‚Äîfrom the famous Libet experiments to modern BCI technology‚Äîto understand when and how conscious intention relates to action.

Core Research Questions

  • Timing of Awareness: When does conscious awareness of intention actually occur relative to neural preparation for action?
  • Sense of Agency: What neural signatures distinguish voluntary from involuntary actions?
  • Veto Power: Can we detect and measure the "free won't"‚Äîthe ability to cancel prepared actions?
  • Real-time Modulation: How does feedback about brain states affect subsequent decision-making and agency?

ü§î Philosophical Context: The Free Will Debate

Historical Background

The question of free will has puzzled philosophers for millennia. Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, in his philosophical writings, identified what he called the "two great mysteries" or "sphinxes" of science:

"We must investigate two great mysteries, which might be called the sphinxes of the science, and which are deep enough to tax our intelligence to its utmost."

‚Äî Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz

The Two Great Sphinxes:

  1. The Problem of Necessity and Contingency: How can events be both necessary (following natural laws) and contingent (able to happen differently)?
  2. The Problem of Free Will: How can human actions be both free and determined by prior causes?

Our Focus: This research addresses the second sphinx‚Äîthe mystery of free will and human agency.

These questions remained purely philosophical until modern neuroscience provided tools to investigate them empirically. The landmark experiments by Benjamin Libet in the 1980s brought these ancient questions into the laboratory.

‚ö° The Libet Experiment: A Neuroscience Revolution

üß† The Brain Decides First

Unconscious neural activity (the "readiness potential") appears hundreds of milliseconds before you feel the urge to act.

ü§î The Paradox

If your brain "knows" before you do, who's really in control? Is your sense of agency an illusion? Or is there something deeper going on?

üî¨ The Setup

Participants watched a clock and noted when they decided to move. Meanwhile, EEG recorded their brain activity, revealing the timing puzzle.

üéØ Our Approach

We're revisiting Libet's findings with modern technology: real-time brain-computer interfaces, machine learning, and more sophisticated neural recording methods. Our goal isn't just to replicate the past, but to understand the mechanisms of voluntary action and test whether conscious awareness can influence processes once thought to be purely unconscious.

üî¨ Experimental Design

Setup & Methodology

Hardware:

  • OpenBCI Cyton board (8-channel EEG)
  • Standard 10-20 electrode placement focusing on motor cortex (C3, C4) and supplementary motor area
  • High-precision timing system synchronized with neural recording

Experimental Protocol:

  • Participants perform self-paced voluntary movements (button presses)
  • Real-time detection of readiness potential (RP) using trained classifiers
  • Participants report timing of conscious intention using modified Libet clock
  • Comparison of neural onset vs. reported awareness timing

Novel Additions:

  • Real-time feedback: Participants see prediction of their intention before they're consciously aware
  • Veto trials: Testing the "free won't" by allowing participants to cancel prepared actions
  • Agency manipulation: Introducing delays or mismatches to disrupt sense of agency
